Design System

Atoms definition

The foundation is pivotal in a design system and requires meticulous planning to ensure it is fail-safe, as components derive their styles from the system's atomic elements to provide functional and beautiful product experience.

In this file I have done a deep dive into throughly explaining the importance of maintaining consistency and well defined foundational elements.

"A design system is a perpetually evolving entity. Putting a final touch on it and declaring it finished proves to be a challenging endeavour.”

Colors

The color atoms are carefully chosen and defined within the system, so that they interact harmoniously across to create a cohesive and consistent aesthetic appearance various components and interfaces.

The colours palette has been divided into four major categories:

  • Grays (neutrals)
  • Primary
  • Accent
    • Red
    • Green
  • System colors

The selection of primary and accent colors adheres to the triadic color palette scheme. The primary color takes center stage emphasizing the main theme, while accent colors complement the primary blue. The color palette is designed with flexibility, allowing the addition of more colors under the well-defined color atom for accents.

Colors are categorized, accessability checked and pre-defined for various elements, encompassing background surfaces, interaction states, and text colors for both grayscale and colored palettes. This clarity in categorization informs the usage and definition of design tokens.

Elevations, Spacers, icons

Elevations and spacers serve as composite tokens within a design system, encapsulating string values that intricately define the appearance of visual elements. Elevations determine the depth and shadow of components, contributing to a sense of hierarchy and depth within the user interface. Spacers, on the other hand, establish consistent spacing between elements, ensuring a harmonious and well-balanced layout.

Icons

Icons are visual symbols that convey specific meanings or actions within the interface. They are categorised into thin and filled icons, each serving distinct purposes. Thin icons offer a lightweight and minimalistic visual representation, ideal for instances where a subtle presence is preferred. Filled icons, on the other hand, provide a bolder and more pronounced appearance, making them suitable for emphasising key actions or features.

To maintain consistency across the user interface, the design system defines standardized icon sizes. These sizes are carefully chosen to ensure icons align seamlessly with other interface elements, creating a cohesive and visually pleasing user experience.

Typography

Typography in design systems involves the systematic and intentional selection and application of fonts and text styles to create a cohesive and visually appealing user interface. Its design tokens are componite in nature encompassing considerations such as font families, sizes, line spacing, and weights. All defined within a design system to ensure consistency and readability across various components and screens.
